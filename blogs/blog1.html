Training from Scratch vs. Fine-Tuning vs. Transfer Learning

Published on September 21, 2025

← Back to Blog List

It has almost been a year since I started my Artificial Intelligence & Machine Learning journey. After many projects and many failures, I realized that some of the biggest lessons aren't found in textbooks, but in trying to build something real. This is the story of my 'Raag Detector' project and what it taught me about training models.

Training a model "from scratch" is exactly what it sounds like. You begin with a model architecture, but its "brain" is a blank slate. All the parameters, or weights, are initialized with random values. My initial attempt with the 'Raag Detector' failed precisely because I was trying this method without meeting any of the prerequisites. A small CNN with a tiny dataset of 300 recordings couldn't possibly learn the incredibly complex features of Indian classical music from random noise.

What it requires:

  • A massive dataset: We're talking hundreds of thousands, if not millions, of data points.
  • Huge computational power: Training can take days, weeks, or even months on powerful GPUs or TPUs.
  • Deep expertise: A solid understanding of model architecture, optimization, and regularization is crucial.

After my initial failure, I realized a crucial concept: why reinvent the wheel? This is the core idea behind Transfer Learning and Fine-Tuning. These techniques leverage a pre-trained model ‚Äì a model that has already been trained on a massive, general dataset (like ImageNet for images or AudioSet for sounds). We can use this "learned knowledge" as a powerful head start.

Transfer Learning: The Expert Consultant üßê

Think of Transfer Learning as hiring an expert consultant. You'd hire an expert biologist who already knows about feathers and beaks; you just need to teach them to recognize the specific birds in your local area. In technical terms, Transfer Learning involves:

  • Taking a pre-trained model (e.g., VGGish or YAMNet).
  • Freezing the early layers to keep their expert knowledge intact.
  • Replacing the final classification layer with a new one for your specific task.

We then train only this new final layer on our smaller dataset. Since most of the network is frozen, training is incredibly fast.

Fine-Tuning: The Specialist's Touch ‚ú®

Fine-Tuning is a more nuanced extension of Transfer Learning. It's like your expert biologist not only learning your local birds but also slightly adjusting their general understanding of "feathers" based on the unique patterns they see.

The process is similar, but instead of freezing all the early layers, you unfreeze some of the later ones. You then train the entire network (both the new layer and the unfrozen pre-trained layers) on your data, but with a very low learning rate. This is crucial‚Äîit ensures the powerful pre-trained weights are only nudged or "fine-tuned" slightly to become more relevant to your specific data, without forgetting their excellent general knowledge.

Revisiting the 'Raag Detector': A New Game Plan

Armed with this new understanding, my approach today would be completely different.

  • Step 1: Find a Pre-trained Audio Model. I would start with a model like PANNs, which has been trained on the massive AudioSet dataset. It already knows what music sounds like.
  • Step 2: Apply Transfer Learning. I would freeze its base layers, add a new classifier for my Raags, and train it on my 300 recordings. This would give me a much better baseline.
  • Step 3: Graduate to Fine-Tuning. If I could collect more data, say a few thousand recordings, I would then try fine-tuning. I would unfreeze the top few layers of the pre-trained model and retrain everything with a low learning rate to specialize its knowledge.

My first attempt wasn't a failure; it was a necessary lesson. It taught me that in machine learning, the goal isn't always to build everything yourself. The smartest path is often to leverage the incredible work that has already been done and adapt it to your unique vision.
